Yili Duan

Observing public culture, participation, and trust in real-world settings.

This site is not a portfolio of outcomes, nor a chronological record of experiences. It is a collection of field-based observations about how people, responsibility, and public culture operate in practice.

I am interested in how individuals learn, adjust, and become trusted in environments that do not primarily exist to support or train them.

Featured Fieldworks

Selected situations where my role was temporary, but responsibility was real.

Battle of Ideas — Worldwrite

This fieldwork confirmed that I can continue learning and functioning in unfamiliar, high-intensity environments without retreating.

The experience was not framed as personal development. Responsibility was temporarily assigned rather than carefully cultivated. Trust emerged through preparation, reliability, and self-regulation.

I observed how public cultural events rely on a large amount of invisible labour, and how learning often takes place under conditions that are efficient rather than gentle.

Hanbury Hall — National Trust

In contrast, this setting required emotional attentiveness and stability, particularly when working with children and families in shared public spaces.

I learned how trust is built not through visibility or technical expertise, but through making others feel safe, understood, and at ease.

These experiences shaped my understanding of responsibility as something relational rather than performative.

How I Observe & Learn

I tend to focus less on abstract argumentation and more on interaction, response, and situational dynamics.

In public discussions, I often read the room through reactions, pauses, and shifts in attention rather than through complete argumentative arcs.

In unfamiliar environments, I rely on asking questions, regulating my own expectations, and allowing space for error without withdrawing from responsibility.

Movement & Travel

When travelling, I tend to remember people encountered along the way and fragments of history shared informally, rather than destinations or landmarks themselves.

These moments often reveal how cultural knowledge is carried through everyday interactions rather than formal narratives.

Background

My academic background informs how I approach observation, particularly in relation to media, culture, and participation.

However, this site prioritises situated experience over formal credentials or chronological progression.
